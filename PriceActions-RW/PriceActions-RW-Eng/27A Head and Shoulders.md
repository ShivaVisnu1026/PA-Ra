# Head and Shoulders (Part One)

## Core Definition and Identification of Head and Shoulders Patterns

-   **Nature**: The head and shoulders pattern is a **major reversal structure**.
    -   **Head and Shoulders Top**: Essentially a "Lower High Major Reversal".
    -   **Head and Shoulders Bottom**: Essentially a "Higher Low Major Reversal".
-   **Key Structure**:
    -   **Prerequisite**: First requires a **strong, valid trendline break**, signaling the original trend may be ending. This is the prerequisite for forming a reliable head and shoulders pattern.
    -   **Core**: After the trendline is broken, the market **attempts to resume the original trend but fails**, forming a **lower high** (for tops) or a **higher low** (for bottoms). This failed attempt point is the **right shoulder**, the focus for traders.
    -   **Significance of Left Shoulder**: Not all major reversals have a left shoulder. The presence or absence of a left shoulder is not the most critical factor for judging pattern validity. The core is the trendline break and formation of the right shoulder.

## Most Head and Shoulders Patterns are "Minor Reversals"

-   **Core View**: Although head and shoulders patterns are defined as major reversal structures, in reality, **the vast majority of head and shoulders patterns will ultimately evolve into trading ranges or complex pullbacks (flags) within trends, rather than launching new, opposite trends**.
-   **Identifying Minor Reversals**:
    -   **Lack of Prerequisite**: If a head and shoulders pattern forms without a prior strong trendline break, it is very likely a minor reversal.
    -   **Too Small**: Pattern contains too few bars, or reversal strength (bar body size, duration) is insufficient, usually also indicating this is just a minor reversal.
-   **Trading Implication**: Even minor reversals may provide a **swing trading** opportunity (e.g., lasting 10 bars, two-legged move), but traders should not overly expect them to start a long-term opposite trend.

## Trading Strategy and Entry Timing

-   **Nature of Trading**: Any head and shoulders pattern can be viewed as a **trading range**. Trading head and shoulders patterns is essentially trading the breakout of this trading range or trading reversals at its boundaries.
-   **Trade-offs Between Three Entry Methods**:
    -   **Early Entry (Low Win Rate, Excellent Reward/Risk)**: Enter at the pattern's extreme points (e.g., buying at the "head" of a head and shoulders bottom). Risk is small, potential return is huge, but success rate is low (approximately 40%).
    -   **Standard Entry (Moderate Win Rate, Good Reward/Risk)**: Enter when the **right shoulder** forms a reversal signal. This is the classic approach for trading "lower highs/higher lows", a balanced choice between win rate and reward/risk.
    -   **Confirmed Entry (High Win Rate, Poor Reward/Risk)**: Wait for price to **strongly break the "neckline"** (i.e., the range boundary) with good follow-through bars before entering. Win rate is higher, but stop is further, potential profit space is also smaller.
-   **Concept of "Neckline"**: The neckline is not a precise line but the support/resistance area of the trading range formed by the pattern. Different traders and algorithms may draw different necklines.

## Summary Principles

-   **Most are Minor Reversals**: Recognize that the vast majority of head and shoulders patterns become trading ranges, not new trends.
-   **Trendline Break is Prerequisite**: A trustworthy head and shoulders reversal signal must be preceded by a **strong break of the original trendline**.
-   **Right Shoulder is Core Signal**: The focus of trading is capturing the opportunity after trend resumption fails to form a **"lower high" or "higher low"** (i.e., the right shoulder).
-   **Context Determines Everything**: Head and shoulders patterns forming within strong channels are likely just complex **pullbacks/flags**; trading with the trend is usually wiser. Only patterns appearing after trend strength clearly weakens have reversal potential.
