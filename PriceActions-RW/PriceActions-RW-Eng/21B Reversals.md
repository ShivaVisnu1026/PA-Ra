# Reversals (Part 2): Trading Characteristics, Entry Timing, and Cup with Handle Pattern

## I. Risk/Reward Characteristics of Reversal Trading

- **Core Trade-off: Risk vs. Win Rate**

  1. **Entering in Early Reversal Stage (e.g., when first reversal signal appears at potential bottom/top)**:
     - **Advantage: Initial risk is relatively small**.
       - Because stop loss can usually be set close to entry point (e.g., beyond the low/high of the reversal bar).
       - If the reversal succeeds and develops into a large trend, potential **reward/risk ratio may be very high**.
     - **Disadvantage: Win rate is relatively low**.
       - In the early stages of reversal, trend direction is not yet clearly changed, so failure probability is high. Many early reversal attempts may ultimately just be minor reversals, evolving into flags or ranges, or even original trend resuming.
       - Al Brooks mentions that even well-formed reversal structures may have initial success rates of only about 40% for directly developing into large-scale counter-trends.

  2. **Entering After Reversal Gets Some Confirmation, New Trend Begins to Show (e.g., breaking neckline, breakout after flag formation)**:
     - **Advantage: Win rate is relatively higher**.
       - Because market direction is clearer and has gained recognition from more market participants.
     - **Disadvantage: Initial risk is usually larger**.
       - Entering at this time, reasonable stop placement may be farther from entry point, causing initial risk amount per trade to increase.

- **Conclusion**: Traders need to choose between "high reward/risk, low win rate" early reversal entries and "lower reward/risk, high win rate" trend-confirmed entries, which usually depends on individual risk tolerance and trading strategy.

## II. Multiple Entry Opportunities During Reversal Process

- **Reversals Are Not Single Entry Points**: A successful trend reversal process usually provides multiple entry opportunities with different risk/reward characteristics.
  - **Earliest Entry**: When first reversal signal appears (such as bullish reversal bar at bottom, second bottom of small double bottom, etc.).
  - **Subsequent Entries**:
    - **Breaking Key Levels**: Such as breaking double bottom/top neckline, breaking down/up trend lines.
    - **Flags/Pullbacks**: When first pullback (bull flag/bear flag) appears during new trend formation and shows breakout signal.
    - **After "Surprise Bar"**: An unexpected, strong same-direction trend bar appears, often indicating trend acceleration. Can enter at its close or subsequent pullback.
    - **High/Low 1, 2, 3, 4 Setups**: During trend development, look for entry points based on price action counting (such as high 2 buy, low 2 sell).
- **"Never Too Late" Philosophy**: Al Brooks emphasizes that as long as a trend (whether newly reversed or continuing) is still healthy, reasonable entry opportunities can usually be found. There's no need to regret missing the initial start point or chase breakouts.

## III. Failure Rate of Breakout Attempts in Trading Ranges (80% Rule Application)

- Al Brooks again mentions his "80% Rule" application in trading ranges: **Within a clear, sustained trading range, approximately 80% of breakout attempts (whether upward testing resistance or downward testing support) will ultimately fail.**
- This means price will likely return to the range interior or move to the other side of the range.
- **Trading Implication**: Within trading ranges, blindly chasing just-occurred breakouts has relatively high risk because failure probability is high. A more prudent strategy may be to wait for failed breakout signals or trade using reversal signals at range edges.

## IV. Classic Reversal Pattern: "Cup with Handle"

- **Definition**: This is a well-known pattern that usually appears in uptrends as a **bullish reversal** (if appearing after long decline) or **bullish continuation** (if appearing within existing uptrend).

- **Pattern Composition**:
  1. **"The Cup"**:
     - Market first experiences a decline (or pullback from prior high), then forms a "U"-shaped or rounded bottom.
     - Price gradually recovers, reaching or approaching the high level where the decline began (the left edge of the "cup").
     - "Cup" formation usually takes considerable time, representing gradual shift in market sentiment and bottom accumulation process. An ideal "cup" should not be too sharp (V-shaped).
  2. **"The Handle"**:
     - When price recovers to the right edge of the "cup" (near prior high), market usually experiences a **relatively small magnitude, relatively short duration pullback or sideways consolidation**.
     - This small pullback or consolidation forms the "handle". Ideal handle depth usually should not exceed 1/3 to 1/2 of the "cup" height, and should form in the upper half of the "cup".

- **Pattern Interpretation**:
  - "Cup" formation, especially its right-side rise, shows strengthening of buying power.
  - "Handle" is viewed as the final **shakeout or washout** by major funds before formal upward breakout, aiming to clear out weak holders and reduce pressure for subsequent rally.

- **Trading Signal**:
  - When price **breaks up through the upper trend line of the "handle"**, or more conservatively, **breaks up through the right edge of the "cup" (i.e., prior high)**, it is usually considered a buy signal.
  - If breakout is accompanied by significant volume increase, signal reliability is higher.

- **Importance and Considerations**:
  - The reliability of "cup with handle" pattern is related to its **timeframe** (more reliable when appearing on longer timeframes such as daily charts), **pattern proportion and symmetry of "cup" and "handle"**, and **price action and volume at breakout**, among other factors.
  - A well-formed, longer-duration (especially "cup" formation time) "cup with handle" pattern usually has greater subsequent rally potential.
