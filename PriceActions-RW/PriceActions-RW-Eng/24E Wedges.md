# Failed Wedges

## Opposite Breakout of a Wedge Flag

### Definition and Characteristics
-   **Concept**: A wedge flag that should signal trend continuation (such as bull flag, bear flag) instead breaks out in the opposite direction, evolving into a trend reversal.
-   **Example**: A pullback in an uptrend forms a wedge bull flag (three-push down channel), but the market does not resume the uptrend upward; instead, it strongly breaks down.
-   **Surprise Move**: This opposite breakout is viewed as a low-probability "surprise" event, usually triggering a strong and fast new trend.

### Trading Implications
-   **Two-Legged Move**: After the breakout occurs, usually expect at least two legs (such as "down-rally-down").
-   **Measured Move**: Traders will calculate a Measured Move target (MM) based on the height of the original wedge (or channel). The profit target originally expected by trend traders now becomes the target for the opposite trend.
-   **Profit-Taking and Reversal**: When price approaches the MM target, breakout traders will begin taking profits, providing potential opportunities for counter-trend traders to look for bounces or reversals.

## Special Pattern: Failure of a "Micro Trendline Break" Wedge

### Definition and Identification
-   **Pattern**: A nearly horizontal wedge formed by three weak breakouts. For example, three pushes down, but each low is only slightly lower than the previous, also called "small break after small break".
-   **Context**: This pattern shows the market repeatedly attempting to resume the original trend but all failing, with force consumed within a narrow range.
-   **High Probability Signal**: Once this pattern is strongly broken in the opposite direction, its probability of reaching the Measured Move target (MM) is very high (at least 60% mentioned in the course).

### Trading Strategy
-   **High Alert**: When this pattern is identified, prepare for a strong opposite breakout.
-   **Decisive Entry**: Once a strong opposite breakout bar appears, can actively enter, targeting the MM based on pattern height.

## Failure of a Wedge Breakout

### Concept: The 50/50 Gamble
-   **Scenario**: The market forms a reversal wedge (such as a wedge top), but the first reversal attempt fails, and price instead breaks through the wedge's extreme point (such as the wedge top's high).
-   **Stops Triggered**: This breakout triggers many stops placed beyond the wedge's extreme points (e.g., buy stops for shorts).
-   **Two Possibilities**: Post-breakout action is a 50/50 probability event:
    -   **50% Probability**: Breakout succeeds, price continues in the breakout direction to the Measured Move (MM) target.
    -   **50% Probability**: Breakout fails, the market forms a "failed breakout" and makes a second reversal attempt (e.g., forming a higher high above the wedge top then declining again).

### Trading Insight
-   **Second Entry Signal**: "Failed breakouts" provide traders with second entry opportunities. When the market attempts to reverse, fails, then attempts again, the second signal is usually more reliable.
-   **Flexible Stop Strategy**:
    -   **Conventional Approach**: Exit when the stop is triggered, wait for a clearer second signal before re-entering.
    -   **Advanced Approach**: If judging the market may be in a trading range with insufficient breakout momentum, some traders use wider stops (e.g., based on MM target) and may add positions when "failed breakout" occurs, betting on reversal.

## Summary Principles
-   **Watch for Surprise Breakouts**: Opposite breakouts of wedge flags are strong reversal signals, usually accompanied by a measurable move target (MM).
-   **Identify High-Probability Patterns**: A horizontal "small break after small break" wedge, if broken in the opposite direction, is a higher-probability trading opportunity.
-   **Understand the 50/50 Nature of Breakouts**: When an obvious stop level (such as wedge top/bottom) is broken, realize this is a 50/50 situation; price may continue forward, or may evolve into a "failed breakout" providing a second entry opportunity.
