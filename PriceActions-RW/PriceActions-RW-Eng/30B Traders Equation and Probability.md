# Mathematical Expectation and Probability

## The Core Trade-off: Probability vs. Reward/Risk

### Three Core Variables
-   **Definition**: Any trade is determined by three variables: **Probability**, **Risk**, and **Profit**.
-   **Simplification**: Usually can be simplified to a trade-off between two core elements: **Win Rate (Probability)** and **Reward/Risk Ratio**.

### Perfect Trades Don't Exist
-   **Core Principle**: The market does not offer "perfect trades" that simultaneously have high win rate and high reward/risk ratio. If you gain one advantage, you must sacrifice the other.
-   **Trade-off Relationship**:
    -   **High Reward/Risk** (small risk, large profit) necessarily accompanies **low win rate**.
    -   **High Win Rate** necessarily accompanies **poor reward/risk ratio** (risk greater than profit).
-   **Lottery Analogy**: Buying lottery tickets has excellent reward/risk ratio (spend $1 for hundreds of millions), but win rate is almost zero. The government selling tickets has extremely high win rate (almost 100% profit), but single-trade reward/risk ratio is terrible.

### Trade-off Examples in Trading
-   **Early Entry (Low Win Rate, High Reward/Risk)**: Enter at potential reversal points with very small stops. Risk is minimal, potential profit is huge, but success probability is low (e.g., 40%), most times will be stopped out.
-   **Confirmed Entry (High Win Rate, Low Reward/Risk)**: Wait for market to show strong breakout and follow-through bars before entering. At this point, probability of trend continuation is high (e.g., 60% or more), but stop distance widens, risk increases, reward/risk ratio worsens.

## Two Trading Styles: Scalping vs. Swing Trading

### Scalping
-   **Characteristics**: Pursues **extremely high win rate** (e.g., 90%).
-   **Cost**: **Extremely poor reward/risk ratio**. Traders risk relatively large amounts to earn tiny profits.
-   **Behavior**: Take quick profits, exit on any profit.

### Swing Trading
-   **Characteristics**: Pursues **excellent reward/risk ratio** (profit target at least 2 times risk).
-   **Cost**: **Win rate is lower** (usually around 40%).
-   **Behavior**: Use a few large wins to cover many small losses. This is Al Brooks' recommended approach for most traders.

## Application in Trading Decisions

### Novice Misconception: Only Look at Risk, Ignore Probability
-   **Phenomenon**: Novice traders are extremely fearful of losses, so they always look for entry points with minimum risk.
-   **Consequence**: They don't realize that **minimum risk** means **minimum probability**. This causes them to frequently have small losses, eventually depleting their accounts.

### Combining Market Context ("Look Left")
-   **Importance**: A seemingly strong breakout, if not considering the overall structure on the left side, may be a trap.
-   **Judgment**: Need to judge whether current price action is the start of a new trend, or merely a wide trading range or a pullback/bounce within a larger trend. This determines the trade's true probability.

### Probability Changes in Pullbacks
-   **50% Pullback Level**: In a trend leg, the 50% pullback level is key.
    -   **Pullback < 50%**: Probability of trend continuation is higher (>50%), but entry risk is larger (stop is far), reward/risk ratio is poor.
    -   **Pullback > 50%**: Probability of trend continuation drops (≈50% or lower), but entry risk is smaller (stop is close), reward/risk ratio is better.

## Summary Principles
-   **Accept Trade-offs**: Deeply understand that **high win rate** and **high reward/risk** cannot coexist; this is the foundation of all trading decisions.
-   **Probability is Paramount**: Successful trades must comprehensively consider all variables, especially **probability**, which novices most easily overlook. Focusing only on risk is the main cause of losses.
-   **Clear Style**: Choose based on your strategy to become a **scalper (high win rate)** or **swing trader (high reward/risk)**. For most people, swing trading is easier to profit from.
-   **Context Analysis**: Always **"look left"**, combining overall market structure to assess the true reliability of current signals.
