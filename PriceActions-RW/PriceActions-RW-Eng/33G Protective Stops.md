# Protective Stop Strategy: Breakeven, Retests, and Scalping

## Breakeven Stops

### Definition and Psychology
-   **Concept**: After a trade generates floating profit, traders move stop orders near their entry price (slightly profitable or breakeven) to prevent profitable trades from turning into losses.
-   **Driving Factor**: This is typically an **emotional** decision, stemming from traders feeling **confused or uneasy** about the market, causing their original trading premise to waver.
-   **Lacks Mathematical Basis**: From technical analysis perspective, stop placement should be determined by price action (e.g., swing highs/lows), unrelated to trader's personal entry price. Breakeven stops are psychologically driven rather than logically driven.

### Reasonable Application Scenarios
-   **Scalping**: When a scalp trade approaches profit target but fails to fill and begins reversing, since remaining profit space is small while potential retracement risk is large, moving stop to breakeven is reasonable risk management.
-   **Second Retest of Entry Price**:
    -   Traders can typically accept market **first** retesting their entry price (normal breakout retest).
    -   But if after trend resumes, market **second** retests entry price, this typically means original trend premise (e.g., strong trend) is no longer valid; market may have transitioned to trading range. At this point, breakeven exit is a reasonable decision based on price action.

## Breakout Pullbacks and Stop Management

### Defense of Key Price Levels
-   **Breakout Starting Point**: The **starting point** of a strong breakout (especially buy climax) becomes a key support/resistance level ("magnet"); market has high probability of returning to test it.
-   **Trader Behavior**:
    -   **Bulls**: Will place limit orders to buy near that starting point, or wait for price to form reversal signals there before buying, to defend that key level.
    -   **Bears**: Hope price breaks below this level, thus invalidating uptrend logic, triggering more stops, pushing market toward trading range or reversal.
-   **Premise Change**: If that key level is effectively broken, traders should adjust strategy, switching from "trend trading" mindset (expecting trend continuation) to "range trading" mindset (selling high, buying low at range edges).

### Stop Strategy
-   **Wide Stop vs. Narrow Stop**: After strong breakout, can place stop below breakout starting point (narrower) or below entire swing's starting point (wider). Wide stops can tolerate "false break" of breakout start but require smaller positions.
-   **Multiple Attempts**: After judging market has entered trading range, traders can attempt trades multiple times at range edges (e.g., near support, after breakout failure reverse to go long), as long as their trading premise (market is ranging) remains valid.

## Scalping Stop Strategy

### Beginner Trap
-   **Losing Pattern**: In narrow trading ranges, using **tight stops** and relying on **stop orders** for scalping is a typical losing strategy. This leads to frequent stops, causing "death by a thousand cuts" losses.
-   **Core Problem**: Scalping profit targets are typically smaller than initial risk, thus requiring extremely high win rates (e.g., 70%-90%) to be profitable, which is unrealistic for the vast majority of traders.

### Professional Scalper Approach
-   **Wide Stops**: Excellent scalpers tend to use **wider stops** (similar to swing traders' stops), leaving sufficient room for volatility.
-   **Scaling In**: They improve average entry price and overall win rate by **scaling in** at more favorable positions. This is a high-skill, high-pressure operation, unsuitable for most people.
-   **Risk**: This strategy essentially trades accepting larger potential risk for high win rates.

## Summary Principles
-   **Breakeven Stops Are Emotional Tools**: Their primary role is coping with uncertainty, not the best strategy based on technical analysis. But they are reasonable in specific situations like "second retest of entry price."
-   **Defend Key Price Levels**: Strong breakout starting points are important bull-bear dividing lines; their test results are key to judging whether trend continues.
-   **Avoid Scalping with Narrow Stops in Narrow Ranges**: This is one of the most common losing patterns. Most traders should focus on swing trades where profit targets far exceed risk.
-   **Professional Scalping Is Counter-Intuitive**: Successful scalpers often use wide stops combined with scaling in, requiring advanced skills and strong psychological resilience; not suitable for beginners to imitate.
