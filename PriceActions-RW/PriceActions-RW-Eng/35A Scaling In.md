# Introduction to Scaling In

## Core Concepts and Premises

### Definition and Target Audience
-   **Concept**: Scaling in refers to dividing a trade's position into multiple parts, entering at different price points sequentially, rather than investing all at once.
-   **Target Audience**: This strategy is suitable for **experienced, consistently profitable** traders. Beginners, due to immature psychology and decision-making ability, should avoid using it.

### Core Trading Trade-off
-   **Advantages**: Scaling in can **improve final trade win rate**.
-   **Cost**: Win rate improvement comes with **increased risk** and **decreased risk-reward ratio**. Traders need to make more decisions, increasing trade complexity.

### Key Premise: Objectivity and Position Management
-   **"Don't Care" Position Size**: When scaling in, must use positions small enough that traders won't experience emotional fluctuations due to floating losses, thus maintaining objectivity and focusing on the chart itself.
-   **Risk Control**: Must plan total risk in advance. Even with multiple additions, total position's final loss should not exceed risk of any normal trade.

## Two Main Types of Scaling In

### Scaling In with Profit
-   **Purpose**: In **strong trends**, maximize profits through with-trend additions.
-   **Method**:
    -   Establish initial position in confirmed strong trend.
    -   As trend continues (e.g., breaking prior highs or successful pullback tests), add positions at reasonable signal points.
    -   There should be sufficient distance between addition points (at least one scalp profit space).
-   **Exit Signal**: When trend's **premise changes** (e.g., transitioning from strong breakout to channel or trading range, exhaustion bars, parabolic wedges appear), immediately reduce or close all positions to protect profits.

### Scaling In at a Loss
-   **Purpose**: Primarily for **trade management**, improving average entry price by adding at more favorable price points, thus having opportunity to exit at **breakeven or small loss**.
-   **Method**:
    -   When initial position shows floating loss, but **basic trading premise remains valid**, can add at pre-planned, more favorable price points.
    -   For example, after buying in strong uptrend encountering deep pullback, as long as key starting point isn't broken, can look for signals to add at pullback lows.
-   **Exit Strategy**: After adding, can place limit orders near new average cost to seek breakeven exit; or close first position at initial entry price (breakeven), letting added portion profit.
-   **Core Principle**: **Strictly prohibit** scaling in at a loss when trading premise has already failed; otherwise it's dangerous "averaging down" behavior.

## Who Uses Scaling In?

-   **Institutions and Long-Term Investors**: Such as Buffett, Carl Icahn, when they favor a company, will continuously buy as stock price falls, lowering average cost.
-   **Retail Investors**: Common **dollar-cost averaging** is a typical scaling in strategy, diversifying risk through regular fixed-amount purchases.
-   **Short-Term and Day Traders**: Actively use profit scaling and loss scaling techniques to maximize profits and manage risk.

## Summary Principles
-   **Position Size Is Key**: Always use positions small enough to "not care" when scaling in, to maintain objectivity and control total risk.
-   **Premise Determines Everything**: Only scale in when original trading logic (e.g., trend continuation) remains valid. Once market behavior changes, immediately reassess or exit.
-   **Clarify Trading Goals**: Understand whether each addition is to expand profits (profit scaling) or to rescue the trade (loss scaling), and adopt corresponding strategies.
-   **Plan in Advance**: Scaling in must never be an impulsive decision. Must plan addition points, position sizes, and final stop location before trading.
