# Wedges (Part Two)

## The Nature and Identification of Wedges

### Imperfect Reality
-   **Concept**: Perfect, clearly defined wedges are rare. Most channels, whether converging or not, are essentially three-push structures and can be viewed as wedges in a broad sense.
-   **Key Point**: Focus on the principle that price has attempted to push in one direction three times and failed, rather than being rigid about perfect geometric shapes. Any structure with three failed attempts has the essence of a wedge.

### Pattern & Probability
-   **Ideal Pattern**: The more ideal and textbook-like a pattern, the easier it is for market participants (including algorithms) to identify and form consensus, thus making reversal success more likely.
-   **Subjectivity**: How to count "pushes" is subjective, but as long as the overall structure can be recognized, the specific division method is not crucial.

## Wedge Variations & Special Patterns

### Truncated Wedge / Failed 3rd Push
-   **Pattern**: The third push fails to make a new high/low (e.g., the third low of a wedge bottom is higher than the second low), forming a "shortfall".
-   **Principle**: The market's anticipation of an upcoming reversal is very strong, causing traders (trend traders entering early, counter-trend traders taking profits early) to front-run, preventing price from making new extremes. This is still a valid three-push failure pattern.

### Failed Double Top/Bottom Breakout
-   **Concept**:
    -   **Failed Upward Double Top Breakout**: The market forms a double top (two upward attempts), then a third upward attempt breaks above the double top high but quickly reverses down. This constitutes a de facto three-push upward failure, functionally equivalent to a **wedge top**.
    -   **Failed Downward Double Bottom Breakout**: The market forms a double bottom (two downward attempts), then a third downward attempt breaks below the double bottom low but quickly reverses up. This constitutes a three-push downward failure, functionally equivalent to a **wedge bottom**.
-   **Trading Implication**: Although the name differs (can also be called triple top/bottom), the fundamental principle is three failed attempts, making it a valid reversal signal.

### "Pseudo-Wedges" in Trading Ranges
-   **Context is Key**: If there is no trend to reverse (i.e., the market is in sideways choppy action), even if a three-push structure appears, it should not be viewed as a trend reversal signal.
-   **Trading Strategy**: This structure signals the continuation of choppy action. Trading opportunities are limited to **scalping** within the range, not swing trading.

## Trading Strategies & Mindset

### Identification & Drawing Lines
-   **Proactive Anticipation**: After seeing two reversal attempts, one can connect the highs/lows and extend the lines to anticipate the potential target area for the third push.
-   **Accept "Overshoot"**: Connecting points 1 and 3 is a common method; point 2 often overshoots this line, which is normal and acceptable.

### The Core of Trading
-   **Principle Over Name**: Don't get stuck on precise pattern naming. The core is understanding price action: the market has attempted to break in one direction multiple times but all have failed, indicating momentum exhaustion in that direction, and price will likely move in the opposite direction.
-   **Simplified Approach**: If a structure is poorly formed and lacks trend context, simply treating it as a "narrow trading range" and applying the corresponding strategy is usually more effective than forcibly calling it a "wedge".

### Focus on Second Entry
-   **Uncertainty After "Shortfall"**: When the third push "shortfalls" (undershoots), the win rate of the first reversal signal is lower, as the market may test the low/high again.
-   **Improving Win Rate**: Waiting for a clearer second entry signal (such as a small double bottom/top, a better signal bar) is usually a safer, higher-probability strategy.
