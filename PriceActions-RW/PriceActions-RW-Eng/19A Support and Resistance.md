# Support and Resistance (Part 1)

## What is Support and Resistance?
- **Support**
  - A price level below the current price.
  - At this level, buying pressure is expected to be stronger than selling pressure, selling decreases or stops, and buying dominates.
  - Price tends to bounce or stop falling at this level.
- **Resistance**
  - A price level above the current price.
  - At this level, selling pressure is expected to be stronger than buying pressure, buying decreases or stops, and selling dominates.
  - Price tends to pull back or stop rising at this level.
- **Pivots / Decision Points**
  - The market faces a decision at support or resistance: reverse or break through.
  - These areas are often accompanied by significant price volatility.

## The Importance of Support and Resistance
- **Price Action Dominates**: Price is primarily controlled by support and resistance, not news events. News is often just coincidence or a catalyst.
- **Impact of Computerized Program Trading**: Due to the prevalence of algorithmic trading, support and resistance levels are often very precise.

## Support and Resistance in Trends (80% Rule Application)
- **Core Viewpoint (80% Rule)**:
  - About 80% of the time, a strong downtrend **will not** stop at (small/obvious) support levels, but will break through them.
  - About 80% of the time, a strong uptrend **will not** stop at (small/obvious) resistance levels, but will break through them.
- **Market Inertia**: Markets tend to continue what they were previously doing.
- **Expectation of Reversal Attempts**:
  - In strong trends, about 80% of reversal attempts will fail.
  - Failed reversal attempts (such as pullbacks/bounces) usually evolve into continuation flags (bear flags/bull flags).
- **Trading Strategy Implications**:
  - **Trend-Following Trading**: In strong trends, don't easily try to catch reversals at potential support/resistance levels.
  - **Breakout Trading**: Expect prior lows/highs to be broken, and enter in the trend direction when breakouts occur.
  - **Flag Trading**: When reversal attempts fail and form flags, enter in the trend direction when flags break.
  - For example, in a downtrend, when price bounces to the downtrend line (resistance) and shows a reversal signal, go short, expecting the bounce to fail and form a bear flag.
  - When a large bear bar closes below a prior low, go short, betting on trend continuation.

## Support and Resistance in Trading Ranges (80% Rule Application)
- **Core Viewpoint (80% Rule)**:
  - When the market is in a trading range, about 80% of upward breakout (testing resistance) attempts will fail.
  - When the market is in a trading range, about 80% of downward breakout (testing support) attempts will fail.
- **Trader Behavior**:
  - Expect breakouts to fail, so tend to sell at the top of the range (resistance) and buy at the bottom of the range (support).
  - Mostly scalping trades, taking small profits and exiting quickly.
- **Trading Strategy Implications**:
  - **Sell High, Buy Low**: Look for selling opportunities at the top of the range and buying opportunities at the bottom of the range.
  - **Focus on Second Entry Signals**: Such as double top bear flags at the top of the range, or double bottom bull flags at the bottom of the range, can improve win rates.

## The Nature of Market Behavior: Searching for Fair Price
- **Market Purpose**: Exists to facilitate trading, always trying to maximize the number and volume of transactions.
- **Searching for Fair Price**: The market constantly searches for a "fair price" that satisfies both buyers and sellers, but the fair price itself is dynamically changing.
- **Price Overshooting and Probing**: Markets often need to "overshoot" up or down to probe price boundaries, then oscillate around a fair price range accepted by the market.
- **The Pull of Support and Resistance**: Support and resistance are like two forces, constantly pulling price back and forth, testing the resolve of both sides (bulls and bears).

## Common Support and Resistance Pattern Examples
- **Prior Highs/Lows**: Formed by multiple reversals at the same price level.
- **Trend Lines and Channel Lines**: Diagonal lines connecting multiple reversal points.
- **Measured Move (MM) Targets**:
  - When a breakout occurs (such as a gap formed by breaking through a prior key level), the market often reaches a target based on doubling that breakout magnitude.
  - MM targets themselves become new support or resistance.
- **Parabolic Wedges and Climax Reversals**:
  - At the end of trends (usually after several pushes), parabolic accelerating moves (wedges) may form, which are often signals of trend exhaustion (selling climax or buying climax).
  - After such patterns, there is a high probability of at least some degree of bounce or entering consolidation.
  - This is a time for shorts/longs to take profits and aggressive counter-trend traders to enter.
  - Often coincides with channel lines, MM targets, and other support/resistance factors.

## Important Trading Principles
- **Timeframe Consistency**: Your trading decisions (including stop placement) must be based on the timeframe you observe and trade.
  - For example, if trading based on monthly bars, stops should be set according to monthly bar volatility and signals, not referring to 5-minute chart short-term fluctuations.
  - Market movements on different timeframes may be completely opposite; focus on your chosen trading timeframe.
- **Signal Relativity**: A simple signal on a large timeframe chart (such as a high 1 buy) may correspond to a complex multi-signal structure on a small timeframe chart. Conversely, a complex pullback on a small timeframe may be just a simple pullback pattern on a large timeframe.
- **Most Fluctuations Caused by Support/Resistance**: Regardless of timeframe, most price fluctuations you see are the result of the market testing support and resistance. The key is identifying these key areas on the current chart.

## Precision of Support and Resistance
- **Computer-Dominated Markets (e.g., E-Mini S&P 500 - ES)**:
  - Due to the dominance of algorithms and program trading, support and resistance levels (especially MM targets, trend lines, channel lines) can be very precise, sometimes even to within 1 tick.
  - Traders' entries and exits are mostly based on mathematical calculations.
  - Learning to identify these widely recognized targets is crucial.

**Core Summary of This Section:**
1. Support is a price level where buying may be stronger than selling; resistance is a price level where selling may be stronger than buying.
2. Price is primarily driven by support and resistance, not news.
3. Set stops according to the timeframe you trade, and focus on that timeframe.
4. In computer program-dominated markets, support and resistance are very precise.
