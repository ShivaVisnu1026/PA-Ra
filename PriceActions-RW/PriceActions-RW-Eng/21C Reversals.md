# Reversals (Part 3): Beginner Mistakes, Buying/Selling Pressure, and Nested Structures

## I. Common Mistakes for Beginners in Reversal Trading

- **Core Problem: Over-focusing on "Low Risk", Ignoring "Low Win Rate" and Overall "Mathematical Expectation"**

  1. **One-sided Pursuit of Low Risk**:
     - Beginner traders usually have small account sizes and weaker ability to bear losses and stronger psychological fear.
     - Therefore, they instinctively tend to look for trading opportunities where **initial stops seem small**, and reversal points (especially trying to catch tops/bottoms early in trends) often allow stops to be set relatively close.

  2. **Ignoring Low Win Rate Reality**:
     - **Key Mistake**: Although the initial risk of a single reversal trade may be low, its **success rate (win rate) is often also low**. Most early reversal attempts will fail, evolving into flags, ranges, or original trend resuming.
     - Beginners may develop path dependence due to a few successful low-risk, high-reward reversal trades, but in the long run, if win rates are not correctly assessed, frequent low-win-rate reversal attempts will cause account losses to persist.
     - **"Reversals Everywhere" Illusion**: Due to psychological preference for reversals, beginners may "create" or "over-interpret" reversal signals on charts, such as constantly looking for every minor pullback as a shorting opportunity in strong uptrends (e.g., thinking every small three-push is a wedge top).

  3. **Failing to Fully Assess Trade Mathematical Expectation (Trader's Equation)**:
     - A complete trading decision should be based on: Risk (R), Reward, Win Rate (Probability of Winning, Pw), and Loss Rate (Probability of Losing, Pl).
     - Mathematical Expectation = (Pw × Average Profit) – (Pl × Average Loss). Only when mathematical expectation is positive can a trading strategy be profitable long-term.
     - Beginners may only see (Low Risk × High Potential Reward) and ignore the cumulative negative impact of (High Loss Rate × Low Risk).

- **Behavioral Manifestations**:
  - Premature and overly frequent counter-trend operations in strong trends.
  - Identifying reversals based only on patterns (such as seemingly "wedge" shapes) without clear buying/selling pressure evidence.
  - Even entering eagerly without waiting for main trend lines to be broken.

## II. Identifying Real Buying and Selling Pressure

- This is key to judging whether reversals have real momentum, far more important than pattern recognition alone.

- **Characteristics of Strong Selling Pressure** (applicable to top reversals or declining continuation):
  1. **Large Bear Trend Bars**: Long bar bodies, closes near daily lows.
  2. **Consecutive Bear Bars**: Several consecutive bear bars appear, and the total volume (sum of body sizes) of these bear bars is significantly greater than the volume of rally bull bars interspersed.
  3. **Short Lower Shadows on Declining Bars**: Indicates sellers dominated during bar formation, buyers had almost no ability to push price back up.
  4. **Breaking Key Support**: Such as effectively breaking below prior lows, important moving averages, or uptrend lines.

- **Characteristics of Strong Buying Pressure** (applicable to bottom reversals or rally continuation):
  1. **Large Bull Trend Bars**: Long bar bodies, closes near daily highs.
  2. **Consecutive Bull Bars**: Several consecutive bull bars appear, and the total volume of these bull bars is significantly greater than the volume of pullback bear bars interspersed.
  3. **Short Upper Shadows on Rally Bars**: Indicates buyers dominated during bar formation, sellers had almost no ability to push price back down.
  4. **Breaking Key Resistance**: Such as effectively breaking above prior highs, important moving averages, or downtrend lines.

- **Beware of "Weak" Reversal Signals**:
  - If reversal attempt bars (e.g., bear bars attempting to go short at tops) have small bodies or very long lower shadows (indicating buyers are supporting below), this shows insufficient real selling pressure.
  - If these reversal signal bars' volumes are far smaller than prior dominant trend bars (e.g., large bull bars in uptrends far larger than small bear bars at tops), reversal reliability is low.
  - In such cases, the market is more likely to just form a **continuation flag**, and the original trend will continue.

## III. Application of Nested Structures in Reversal Analysis

- **Definition**: Refers to a smaller timeframe or smaller-scale chart structure that is nested within a larger timeframe or larger-scale structure of the same or related type.

- **Examples**:
  - A small **wedge top** may itself be the **third push in a larger wedge top**.
  - A **double bottom** on a 5-minute chart may happen to form at a key **support level** on a daily chart or the **50% retracement** of a prior rally.
  - The low of the right shoulder of a small-level **head and shoulders bottom** may be exactly the touch point of a larger-level **uptrend line**.

- **Analytical Significance and Importance**:
  1. **Signal Resonance and Enhancement**: When bullish or bearish signals of different levels and types converge in the same key area ("resonance effect"), the reliability and importance of reversal in this area will significantly increase. For example, if near a strong support level on a daily timeframe, a clear bullish reversal pattern (such as morning star, piercing pattern) appears on a 1-hour chart simultaneously, accompanied by increased volume, the probability of bullish reversal is far higher than a single signal.
  2. **Providing Multi-dimensional Confirmation**: Identifying nested structures helps traders examine potential reversal opportunities from multiple levels, avoiding decisions based solely on single, isolated signals.
  3. **Assisting in Judging Reversal Levels**: Reversal signals within larger-level contexts usually trigger larger-level moves; conversely, isolated reversals appearing only on small timeframe charts may have limited impact only on small levels.
  4. **Focusing on Patterns at Key Levels**: When price moves to important prior highs/lows, trend lines, channel lines, moving averages, Fibonacci retracement/extension levels, or even measured move (MM) targets, if reversal patterns with nested characteristics form simultaneously, high attention should be given.

## IV. Trading Implications Summary

- **Comprehensive Assessment**: Successful reversal trading requires comprehensive assessment of risk, potential reward, and win rate. Don't blindly enter just because initial risk seems small.
- **Pressure is Key**: Identifying real buying/selling pressure behind bars is core; patterns are only appearance.
- **Wait for Confirmation**: For beginners, waiting for clearer signals (such as trend line breaks, key resistance/support breaks, bar patterns accompanied by real buying/selling pressure) is usually safer than prematurely guessing reversals.
- **Understand Nesting**: Learning to identify and utilize nested structures can help traders find reversal opportunities at more reliable locations and improve understanding of market structure depth.
- **Beginner Strategy**: For less experienced traders, reducing frequency of counter-trend reversal-catching in strong trends and learning more about pullback entries or flag breakouts in trend-following trades may be better ways to accumulate experience and protect accounts.
