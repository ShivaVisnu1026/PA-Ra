# Actual Risk and Profit Targets

## Actual Risk and Profit Targets

### Definition Distinction
-   **Initial Risk**: Maximum acceptable risk set at entry based on reasonable stop point (e.g., signal bar low, swing starting point).
-   **Actual Risk**: After entry, the maximum unfavorable retracement distance before price moves in your favor. **Actual risk is always less than or equal to initial risk**. Often, market moves directly as expected, resulting in very small actual risk.

### Mathematical Reasonableness vs. Optimal Choice
-   **Basic Principle**: Setting profit target at **2 times actual risk** can build a trading system with positive mathematical expectation; this is the **minimum requirement** for profitability.
-   **"D Grade" Analogy**: Meeting minimum requirements (e.g., barely passing in college) doesn't equal best strategy. Under favorable conditions, should pursue higher returns.
-   **High-Probability Trades**: In strong trends and other high win rate trades, can set target at **1 times actual risk** (for scalping), but this is also a minimum target, not the best choice.

## When to Exceed "Minimum Requirements"

### Target Setting in Strong Trends
-   **Problem**: When **actual risk** is very small (e.g., no pullback after breakout), profit targets based on it become negligible, causing you to miss large moves.
-   **Solution**: In high-probability contexts like strong breakouts, should use other methods to set targets rather than relying on tiny actual risk.
-   **Alternative Targets**:
    -   **Measured Move (MM)**: Such as doubling the breakout bar body or entire breakout swing height, typically has high (e.g., 60%) probability of being reached.
    -   **Prior Key Highs/Lows** or other **support/resistance levels**.
    -   Multiples based on **initial risk**.

### Trading Context Determines Strategy
-   **Strong Trend Characteristics**: Strong breakout, little bar overlap, good follow-through. Should boldly hold, pursuing MM and other larger targets.
-   **Trend Weakens/Enters Trading Range**: When trend shows fatigue (e.g., consecutive dojis, increased bar overlap, narrow channel formation), should switch to trading range thinking, more inclined to take profits or at least partial profits near range tops/bottoms and other resistance levels.

## Understanding Outliers and Sample Error in Trading

### About Large Losses in Scalping
-   **Scenario**: Traders may encounter a trade expecting small profit (e.g., 10 pips) but unexpectedly large loss (e.g., 50 pips).
-   **Analysis**:
    -   This is typically an **outlier**, not representative of strategy's general performance.
    -   Such trades' entry points may themselves be problematic (e.g., chasing highs at weak trend end or market already in trading range).
    -   Profitable strategies have both outlier large losses and necessarily outlier large profits to offset them.

### Sample Error
-   **Core Idea**: Cannot judge a strategy's effectiveness based on single, or even a few extreme trades.
-   **Correct Assessment**: Must judge based on **average performance** and **typical samples** from large numbers of trades, ensuring overall **mathematical expectation** is positive. Don't negate entire system's mathematical soundness due to individual cases.

## Summary Principles
-   **Minimum Standard**: Profit target should be at least **2 times actual risk** (or 1 time in high win rate situations); this is the foundation for building profitable systems.
-   **Exceed Minimum**: When **actual risk** is too small and market context is strong, decisively abandon tiny targets based on it, instead use **MM targets** or other key resistance levels, pursuing larger profits.
-   **Context Is King**: Target setting and holding strategy must flexibly adjust with market context (strong trend vs. trading range).
-   **Objective View of Profits and Losses**: Understand and accept existence of **outliers**; must assess strategies from overall mathematical expectation of large numbers of trades, not be swayed by single trade results.
