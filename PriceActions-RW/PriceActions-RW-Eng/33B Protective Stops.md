# Protective Stops (Part 2)

## Response Strategy After Stop Is Triggered

### Reassessment and Re-entry
-   **Stop Is Not the End**: Being stopped out does not mean the trading idea is completely wrong. This is especially common in breakout trades, where breakout success and failure probabilities are typically 50/50.
-   **Premise Still Valid**: If after being stopped out, market action proves your original trading premise (e.g., market is at a top or bottom area) is still valid, actively look for re-entry opportunities.
-   **Example**: Shorting at a wedge top, stopped out by upward false breakout, if market quickly reverses down and forms strong bear bars, this is an excellent re-entry short signal.

### Wide Stops and Holding
-   **Expert Strategy**: Experienced traders, when expecting market reversal but timing is uncertain, may use very wide stops (and reduce position size) to withstand the final push up/down before reversal, thus capturing the entire swing.
-   **Risk Management**: This strategy requires deep understanding of market structure and risk control through reduced position size; not suitable for beginners.

## Reasonable Stop Placement Selection and Management

### Stop Placement Based on Price Structure
-   **Core Principle**: The most reasonable stop should be placed at a price point that can "falsify" your trading premise.
-   **Breakout Markets**: For strong breakout markets, the most reasonable stop is the starting point of the breakout swing (important major trend low/high). If price returns to that point, the "strong breakout" premise is no longer valid.
-   **Signal Bar Stop**: For entries based on specific signal bars, stops can be placed beyond that bar's high or low. This is the method beginners should prioritize.

### Trailing Stops
-   **Definition**: As trend continues, as market continuously makes new highs/lows, you can gradually move stop to below/above new, **important** swing lows/highs.
-   **Purpose**: Lock in existing profits while leaving sufficient room for trend development.
-   **Key**: Only move stops after new **important** support/resistance levels form, not arbitrarily.

## The Worst Stop: "The Stinky Stop"

### Definition and Psychological Causes
-   **Concept**: A stop that is neither wide enough (cannot withstand normal pullbacks) nor narrow enough (causing excessive risk), typically placed near **minor**, structurally insignificant lows/highs.
-   **Psychology**: Usually stems from trader fear and panic. When profits retrace, traders cannot bear the pressure and urgently move stops up to "protect" profits, but place stops at extremely vulnerable positions easily hit.

### Trading Trap
-   **Two-Legged Pullbacks**: Market pullbacks often occur in two legs (or multiple legs). Placing stops below the first leg's low will likely be hit during the second leg, which is precisely when the trend is about to resume.
-   **Buy High, Sell Low**: This emotionally-based stop adjustment ultimately causes traders to sell at trading range bottoms (where professional traders are buying), causing unnecessary losses.

## Differences Between Beginner and Expert Stop Strategies

### Beginner Strategy
-   **Focus**: Should focus on risk-controllable, clearly defined stop opportunities.
-   **Method**: Use tight stops based on signal bars, so risk is clear and limited.
-   **Discipline**: Must avoid arbitrarily adjusting stops due to emotional fluctuations, especially avoiding "stinky stops."

### Expert Strategy
-   **Flexibility**: Able to use wider stops based on market structure to capture larger moves.
-   **Risk Management**: Control total risk through **reduced position size**, ensuring single-trade risk remains controllable.
-   **Advanced Techniques**: May combine **scaling in** with wide stops, requiring extensive experience and strict money management; beginners should not imitate.

## Summary Principles
-   **Stops Are Part of Trading**: After being stopped out, calmly analyze; if trading premise hasn't changed, look for re-entry opportunities.
-   **Stops Must Be Reasonable**: Stop placement should be based on market structure, placed at key points that can "falsify" your trading logic.
-   **Beware of 'Stinky Stops'**: Avoid placing stops at minor, emotion-based rather than logic-based positions; this is often the source of losses.
-   **Position Size Matches Stop**: Choosing wide stop strategies must correspondingly reduce position size to control total risk.
