# Swing Trading vs. Scalping: Definitions, Management, and Psychology

## Core Differences Between Swing Trading and Scalping

### Definitions and Goals
-   **Swing Trade**
    -   **Core Goal**: Pursue profits at least **twice the initial risk (2R)**.
    -   **Holding Characteristics**: Willing to hold through pullbacks, aiming to capture at least a **two-legged** complete move.
    -   **Holding Time**: Typically hope to hold for **10-20 bars** or longer.
-   **Scalp**
    -   **Core Goal**: Profits less than twice the risk, pursuing quick profits.
    -   **Holding Characteristics**: Typically exit when the first price push ends or upon encountering minor pullbacks, **strongly averse to profit retracements**.

### Win Rate and Mathematical Expectation
-   A reasonable swing trading setup, even with only a 40% win rate, can be profitable long-term as long as it maintains a 2:1 risk-reward ratio, resulting in positive mathematical expectation.

## Key Trade Management Rule: "Second Test of Entry" Principle

### Concept
-   After entering a trade, when price moves in your favor and then **first pulls back to your entry price (test of entry)**, this is usually normal and you can continue holding.

### Warning Signal
-   If price **returns to your entry price a second time** after moving in your favor again, this is a strong signal of trend weakening.

### Trading Implications and Response
-   **Market State Change**: "Second test of entry" indicates the market has likely transitioned from a **trend to a trading range**.
-   **Strategy Adjustment**: The original swing trading premise (betting on trend continuation) is no longer valid. Traders should exit immediately (e.g., breakeven) or switch mindset from "swing" to "scalp," lowering profit expectations and preparing to exit at any time.

## Pattern Analysis and Market Context

### Multiple Signal Confluence
-   When multiple bullish/bearish patterns appear simultaneously on the chart (such as **wedge flags, double bottom/top flags, triangles**), and their entry points roughly coincide, the trade's success rate increases significantly.

### Trend Transition Periods and Trading Ranges
-   **"Big rally, big selloff, big confusion"**: When a trend ends and enters a transition period, the market typically becomes two-sided and uncertain, suitable for scalping or range trading strategies.
-   **Trend Overlap**: A new trend (e.g., downtrend) often begins to form before the old trend (uptrend) completely ends (i.e., before the final new high is made).

### Importance of Multiple Timeframe Perspective
-   What appears as a fierce one-sided trend on a small timeframe (e.g., 5-minute chart) may merely be a **test of an important support/resistance level** (e.g., trading range bottom) on a larger timeframe (e.g., 240-minute chart).
-   Combining larger timeframe context helps assess the true strength of small timeframe "trends." For example, a series of sell climaxes occurring at a daily chart support level is more likely a sign of exhaustion reversal rather than trend continuation.

## Summary Principles
-   **2R as the Boundary**: Use "whether profit target is twice the risk" as the core standard to distinguish swing trading from scalping.
-   **Beware of 'Second Test of Entry'**: Price returning to entry a second time is an important signal of trend transitioning to a trading range; strategy must be adjusted.
-   **Utilize Multiple Timeframes**: Use larger timeframe charts to assess the true context of current price action, avoiding chasing "false trends" on small timeframes near key support/resistance levels.
-   **Understand Stop Logic**: The key to a downtrend is **continuously lower highs**; shorts place stops above important highs, and once broken, the trend premise is shaken. The reverse applies.
