# Wedges (Part Three)

## Beware of Invalid Wedges: Traps in Strong Trends

### Characteristics of Invalid Wedges
-   **Strong Trend Context**: When the market is in a very tight channel (equivalent to a breakout phase), even if three pushes can be counted, it should not be viewed as a reversal signal. This pattern is usually just a strong trend bar on a higher timeframe.
-   **Gap Exists (No Overlap)**: In a downtrend, if the bounce high fails to retrace above the breakout point of the previous decline leg (i.e., there is no overlap between legs), this indicates the downtrend is too strong and going long is extremely risky. The same applies to uptrends.
-   **Lack of Opposing Force**: In a strong trend, the absence of strong opposite signal bars (such as consecutive, full-bodied bull bars in a strong downtrend) indicates weak opposing force, insufficient to trigger a reversal.
-   **Asymmetric Structure**: A reliable wedge should have some symmetry in time and price amplitude between pushes. If the size difference between pushes 1, 2, and 3 is too large, the pattern's reliability decreases.

### Trading Implications
-   **Only Trade with Trend**: In strong trends with the above characteristics, counter-trend trading should be abandoned. Any opposite movement is very likely just a temporary pullback or flag, an opportunity to add to trend positions, not the start of a reversal. 80% of counter-trend attempts will fail.
-   **Reset the Count**: When a strong breakout occurs in a trend, even if a seemingly near-complete pattern (such as the 2nd push) had already formed, traders should "reset the count", treating this strong breakout as the "1st push" of a new trend.

## Transition Phase of Trends

### Overlap of Old and New Trends
-   **Concept**: Trend transitions are usually not instantaneous but occur through a "transition phase". In this phase, a new trend (such as a pullback channel) begins to form before the old trend fully ends.
-   **Example**: In an uptrend, a downward pullback channel (future bull flag or wedge bull flag) may begin its "1st push" before the market makes its final new high. This means the uptrend and the pullback's downtrend "coexist" for a period.
-   **Trading Insight**: Understanding this phenomenon helps identify early signs of pullback patterns (such as wedge flags, High 2/Low 2). Regardless of what this pattern is called, the core is recognizing it as a pullback and preparing to enter with the trend when it resumes.

## Using Multiple Timeframes for Early Entry

### From Higher to Lower Timeframes
-   **Method**: When identifying a potential, near-complete wedge reversal pattern on a larger timeframe (such as monthly, 60-minute charts), switch to a smaller timeframe (such as daily, 5-minute charts) to find earlier, more precise entry signals.
-   **Application**: On smaller timeframe charts, clearer micro reversal structures may appear, such as small double bottoms/tops, small wedges, or strong signal bars, allowing earlier entry.

### Advantages and Disadvantages
-   **Advantages**: Can enter at better prices, reducing initial stop distance and improving potential reward/risk ratio.
-   **Warning**: Although early entry has advantages, frequently switching timeframes can confuse traders. For most people, focusing on a single trading chart and mastering price action on it often yields better long-term performance.

## Summary Principles
-   **Context Over Pattern**: A wedge's trading value depends on its market context. In strong trends, tight channels, with gaps, wedges will likely fail.
-   **Trend Transitions are a Process**: Recognizing the overlapping transition period between old and new trends helps better understand the formation process of pullbacks and flags.
-   **Multiple Timeframes Can Optimize Entry**: With the context of large price structures, using smaller timeframe charts can find lower-risk entry points, but this requires skill and is not suitable for everyone.
