# Triangles (Part One)

## Definition and Characteristics of Triangles

### Broad Definition
-   **Concept**: Any sideways trading range containing at least 3 upward or downward pushes (reversals). Its nature is a "consensus area" where bull and bear forces reach balance.
-   **Minimum Requirement**: Three pushes in one direction means at least 2 pushes in the opposite direction, totaling at least 5 legs.
-   **Identification Key**: No need to be rigid about precise line drawing or naming; recognizing that the market has entered "narrow consolidation" and "bull-bear balance" is the core.

### Main Patterns
-   **Converging Triangle**: The most common pattern, with lower highs and higher lows, gradually narrowing range.
-   **Ascending/Descending Triangle**: One boundary line is essentially horizontal, the other is sloped.
-   **Micro Patterns**: Consecutive inside bar patterns (such as ii, iii) on smaller timeframe charts are essentially small converging triangles.

## Trading Implications of Triangles (Breakout Mode)

### Breakout Mode
-   **Core Nature**: Triangles are typical **breakout mode** structures, meaning the market is building force, waiting for directional choice.
-   **Probabilities**:
    -   For **horizontal** triangles, probabilities of upward and downward breakouts are essentially **50/50**.
    -   The first attempted breakout has a **50% probability of failing**, with price returning to the trading range.

### The "20-Bar" Rule
-   **Short Flags (< 20 bars)**: If a triangle forms within a trend and lasts a short time (less than 20 bars), it is usually a **continuation flag** (such as bull flag/bear flag), with higher probability of breaking in the trend direction.
-   **Neutral Range (> 20 bars)**: Once consolidation lasts more than 20 bars, the influence of the prior trend significantly diminishes. The market enters true **neutral state**, when upward or downward breakout probabilities are equal (50/50).

### Final Flag
-   **Concept**: When a triangle appears at the end of a mature trend, it is likely a **final flag**.
-   **Trading Implication**: Breakouts from final flags are often **exhaustion** moves; the move doesn't go far, and the failure probability is high. After failure, price usually retests the triangle's apex and may trigger a trend reversal.

## Wedges: A Special Sloping Triangle

### Definition and Identification
-   **Concept**: Structurally also containing 3 pushes, but the overall pattern is not horizontal, instead a converging channel that slopes **upward or downward**.
-   **Core Difference from Triangles**: Wedges have strong directional bias for breakouts.

### Trading Implications
-   **Breakout Direction**: Wedges have approximately **~75%** high probability of breaking in the **opposite direction** of their slope.
-   **Specific Application**:
    -   **Falling Wedge**: Treated as a **bull flag**, likely to **break up**, a potential reversal long signal.
    -   **Rising Wedge**: Treated as a **bear flag**, likely to **break down**, a potential reversal short signal.

## Summary Principles
-   **Identification Relies on Pushes**: The key to identifying triangles is finding at least **3 pushes/reversals in the same direction** within sideways consolidation.
-   **Time Determines Probability**: Use **20 bars** as the dividing line to judge whether the pattern is a continuation flag (<20 bars, higher trend probability) or neutral breakout mode (>20 bars, 50/50 probability).
-   **Slope Determines Direction**: Horizontal triangles are 50/50; **sloping wedges** have high probability of breaking in the opposite direction.
-   **Context is Key**: Must judge in conjunction with the trend stage. Triangles at the end of trends are likely **final flags**; beware their breakouts are traps.
