# Order Types and Price Action (Part 3)

## Choosing Order Types Based on Market State

### Strong Trend Markets
-   **Characteristics**: Consecutive large bull/bear bars appear, trend is strong, bars overlap little, tails are short.
-   **Trading Strategy**:
    -   **Multiple Orders Effective**: Stop orders, limit orders, and market orders can all be used.
    -   **With-Trend Trading**: Traders can use stop orders or market orders to chase rallies/selloffs, or place limit orders at small pullbacks in trends (betting on pullback failure).

### Trading Range Markets
-   **Characteristics**: Bars repeatedly reverse, overlap significantly, have long tails, lack clear direction.
-   **Trading Strategy**:
    -   **Beginners Should Wait**: Most traders should avoid trading in such conditions, patiently waiting for clear breakouts.
    -   **Experts Use Limit Orders**: Experienced traders will use limit orders for counter-trend scalping (selling high, buying low), betting on range boundary breakouts failing.
    -   **Stop Order Trap**: Should absolutely not use stop orders, because in trading ranges, breakout attempts have high probability of failing and reversing.

## With-Trend vs. Counter-Trend Limit Orders

### With-Trend Limit Orders
-   **Core Logic**: Bet that pullbacks/retracements in trends will fail and original trend will resume. This is a higher-probability with-trend trading method.
-   **Application Scenarios**:
    -   **Uptrend**: In strong breakout pullbacks, place limit buy orders near pullback bar lows.
    -   **Downtrend**: In strong breakdown bounces, place limit sell orders near bounce bar highs.
-   **Premise**: Market has shown clear trend characteristics, with reason to believe there will be at least a second leg.

### Counter-Trend Limit Orders
-   **Core Logic**: Bet that current trend is weakening and about to fail, transitioning to a trading range.
-   **Application Scenarios**:
    -   When trend shows weak characteristics (e.g., poor follow-through, wedge patterns, bars repeatedly overlapping), begin looking for counter-trend opportunities.
    -   **Sell High**: Place limit sell orders to short at weak uptrend channel tops, potential double tops, or prior key highs.
    -   **Buy Low**: Place limit buy orders to go long at weak downtrend channel bottoms, potential double bottoms, or prior key lows.
-   **Trading Target**: Primarily scalping, or expecting market to enter two-sided trading range, then capturing larger counter-trend swings.

## Using Smaller Timeframes for Early Entry

### Problem and Motivation
-   **Risk Dilemma**: When strong breakouts appear on larger timeframes (e.g., 5-minute chart), if entering directly, stop would be set below breakout start, causing excessive capital risk, and traders often miss opportunities due to fear.

### Solution and Advantages
-   **Switch Timeframes**: Switch to smaller timeframes (e.g., 1-minute chart) to observe the same move.
-   **Early Entry**: On 1-minute chart, you'll see trend formation signs earlier (e.g., consecutive strong trend bars), allowing entry before 5-minute bar closes.
-   **Reduce Risk**: Entering on smaller timeframe, stop (e.g., small pullback low on 1-minute chart) is typically closer than stop on larger timeframe, significantly reducing capital risk per trade, making trades more feasible.

### Trading Implications
-   This is an advanced technique suitable for experienced traders. Beginners may become confused by frequently switching timeframes; it's recommended to focus on a single timeframe first.

## Summary Principles
-   **Price Action Determines Orders**: Order type selection (stop orders vs. limit orders) must first determine whether current market is trending or ranging.
-   **Dual Nature of Limit Orders**: Limit orders can be used both with-trend (betting on pullback failure) and counter-trend (betting on trend failure).
-   **Beginners Prefer Stop Orders**: Beginners should focus on using stop orders for entry in clear trends and always set protective stops.
-   **New Risk Management Approach**: Experienced traders can observe smaller timeframes to find lower-risk early entry points, optimizing risk-reward ratios.
