# Brooks Trading Course: Core Terminology & Basic Concepts

## Core Terminology & Abbreviations

### Common Abbreviations
-   **AIL / AIS**: **Always In Long / Always In Short** - A clear market bias in one direction where traders should only consider trading with that direction.
-   **BO**: **Breakout** - Price moving beyond support or resistance levels.
-   **PB**: **Pullback** - A temporary pause or counter-move within a trend.
-   **TR / TTR**: **Trading Range / Tight Trading Range**.
-   **MTR**: **Major Trend Reversal** - A fundamental change in trend direction.
-   **EMA20**: **20-period Exponential Moving Average** - The only moving average used by default in Al Brooks' course.
-   **H/L 1, 2**: **High/Low 1, 2** - First and second entries with the trend after a pullback, key trading patterns.
-   **TBTL**: **Ten Bars, Two Legs** - A common structure when the market enters a correction phase.

### Basic Units
-   **Tick / Pip**: The smallest price movement unit (commonly used in futures and forex respectively).

## Market Structure & Cycles

### Two States
-   At any given time, the market is either in a **Trend** or in a **Trading Range**.

### Cycle Evolution
-   **Initiation**: Trends usually begin with a strong **Breakout** bar.
-   **Development**: Subsequently evolves into a weaker **Channel** phase.
-   **Termination**: Eventually, the channel often evolves into a **Trading Range**, awaiting the next directional breakout.

## Key Price Action Concepts

### Support and Resistance
-   **Support**: Area below price where downward movement may pause or reverse.
-   **Resistance**: Area above price where upward movement may stall or reverse.

### Bar Reading
-   **Trend Bars**: Large body, small tails, representing clear directional strength.
-   **Trading Range Bars / Doji**: Small body, long tails, representing hesitation and two-way trading.

### Key Bar Patterns
-   **Inside Bar**: A bar whose high and low are completely contained within the previous bar, representing contraction and pause.
-   **Outside Bar**: A bar whose high and low completely engulf the previous bar, representing expansion and potential reversal.

## Trade Entry & Analysis Methods

### Pullback Counting & With-Trend Entries
-   **Core Concept**: In trends, look for pullbacks (or rallies) as entry opportunities.
-   **High 2/Low 2 Signals (H2/L2)**: The second attempt to resume the original direction in a trend (like a double bottom bull flag/double top bear flag), offering high-probability with-trend entries. Also commonly referred to as **ABC Pullbacks**.

### The Importance of Context
-   **Definition**: All price action to the left of the current bar constitutes the "context."
-   **Application**: The validity of trading signals depends heavily on their context. A buy signal in a strong bear trend will likely fail.

### Types of Reversals
-   **Minor Reversal**: Usually just a pullback/rally within a trend (like bear flags/bull flags), with the original trend likely to continue.
-   **Major Trend Reversal (MTR)**: A strong counter-trend breakout with good follow-through, sufficient to change the overall market trend, such as a **Head and Shoulders Bottom** or **Higher Low Major Trend Reversal (HLMTR)**.

### Trading Styles
-   **Scalping**: Quick in and out, typically exiting within 1-5 bars for profit.
-   **Swing Trading**: Holding positions through multiple pullbacks, aiming to capture most of the trend move, typically holding 15-20+ bars.
-   **Investing**: Long-term swing trading on higher timeframes (daily, weekly charts).

## Summary Principles
-   **Market State**: Always determine whether the market is in a trend or trading range - this is the foundation of all analysis.
-   **Context is King**: Any trading signal must be evaluated within its context; signals without context are meaningless.
-   **High Probability Signals**: Focus on and utilize High 2/Low 2 pullback patterns in trends as with-trend entry points.
-   **Core Tool**: EMA20 is the key dynamic support and resistance reference.
-   **Algorithm Dominated**: Recognize that modern markets are controlled by institutional algorithms, and price action is the visual manifestation of their collective behavior.
