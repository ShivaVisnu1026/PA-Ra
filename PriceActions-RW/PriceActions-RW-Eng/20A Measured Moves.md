# Measured Move Targets (Part 1): Leg 1 = Leg 2

## Core Concept: Leg 1 = Leg 2 Equal Amplitude Measurement

- **Definition**: After the market experiences an initial trending move (called "Leg 1"), it usually experiences a pause (such as a pullback, consolidation, or flag), and then the magnitude of the second trending move ("Leg 2") that follows is often roughly equal to the magnitude of Leg 1.
- This is a commonly used expected target level by traders (including algorithmic programs).

## Application Scenarios and Observations of "Leg 1 = Leg 2" MM

1. **After Strong Breakouts**:
   - When the market shows a strong breakout (e.g., breaking through key prior highs/lows, trend lines, or consolidation patterns like wedge flags), traders will expect at least a second leg continuation move comparable to the initial breakout strength (Leg 1).

2. **After Pullbacks/Bounces in Trends**:
   - In already established uptrends or downtrends, when price experiences a pullback or bounce and the trend resumes, subsequent moves often form a second leg similar in magnitude to the trend segment before the pullback/bounce (Leg 1).

3. **Nested Structures**:
   - Large "Leg 1 = Leg 2" patterns may contain smaller-level "Leg 1 = Leg 2" structures. This means a large trend leg may consist of several small sub-legs that conform to the equal amplitude measurement principle.

4. **Application in Spike and Channel**:
   - When a sharp breakout move (Spike) appears, followed by a relatively gentle channel, this sharp breakout itself can be considered Leg 1. If the channel doesn't last long and is broken (usually in the direction of the Spike), a second leg equal amplitude move based on the Spike magnitude may occur.
   - However, if the channel lasts a long time, or bars within the channel perform poorly (e.g., small bull bar bodies, long upper shadows, or many bear bars in an uptrending channel), the market may evolve into a trading range, and the original MM target may not be reached.

## Trading Strategy Implications

1. **Profit Target**:
   - For trend-following traders, after Leg 1 appears and experiences a pullback/consolidation, if expecting the trend to continue, the price level calculated by "Leg 1 = Leg 2" can be used as an important potential profit target.

2. **Counter-Trend Scalping Opportunities**:
   - When price moves near the expected "Leg 1 = Leg 2" MM target, because some trend-following traders will take profits and close positions there, price may show brief stagnation, pullback, or minor reversal in this area. This provides scalpers with potential counter-trend opportunities (e.g., going short near uptrend MM targets, going long near downtrend MM targets).

## Determining the Start/End Points of Leg 1 and Leg 2

- **Subjectivity and Flexibility**: Determining the start and end of Leg 1, and the start of Leg 2, sometimes involves a degree of subjectivity. Different traders or algorithmic programs may choose slightly different reference points:
  - **Leg 1 Start**: May be the absolute lowest/highest point of the leg, or the starting point after breaking through a consolidation area, or the open of a key breakout bar.
  - **Leg 1 End / Leg 2 Start**: Usually where the Leg 1 trend ends and pullback or consolidation begins.
- **Market Reaction is Key**: Rather than obsessing over the "only correct" way to draw lines, focus on the market's actual reaction near different potential MM targets. If price shows obvious stagnation, reversal, or increased volume near a target calculated this way, it indicates that target is being watched by the market (many traders and algorithms).
- **From Near to Far**: Usually first focus on MM targets calculated from the most recent, most obvious legs. If the market easily breaks through that target, then consider more distant targets calculated from earlier or longer legs.

## Notes and Market Behavior Interpretation

- **Target Precision**: MM targets provide an "expected area", not an absolutely precise point. Price sometimes slightly exceeds the target, sometimes may turn before fully reaching it.
- **Market Strength Signals**:
  - If the market is very strong, it may effortlessly break through the first calculated MM target. This usually means trend strength is very high, and traders should expect price to continue toward the next (possibly calculated from larger-level legs) MM target.
  - Conversely, if price shows weakness or reversal far before reaching the MM target, it may indicate insufficient momentum in the original trend or that market structure has changed.
- **Multiple Target Confluence**: If a price area is simultaneously a convergence point of targets calculated by multiple different methods (e.g., "Leg 1 = Leg 2", MM based on prior trading range height, or MM based on a breakout bar body, etc.), the importance of this area as support or resistance will significantly increase.
- **MM Target Failure**: Not all "Leg 1 = Leg 2" patterns will perfectly materialize. Changes in market conditions (e.g., transitioning from trend to wide trading range) may cause expected MM targets to fail. If reversal signals are strong, traders should adjust expectations.

## Same Principles Apply to Downtrends

- All the above principles and observations about "Leg 1 = Leg 2" MM targets in uptrends also apply to equal amplitude measurement target analysis in downtrends.
