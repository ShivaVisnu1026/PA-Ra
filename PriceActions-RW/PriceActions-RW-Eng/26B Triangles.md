# Triangles (Part Two): Expanding Triangles and Pattern Quality

## Expanding Triangles

### Definition and Characteristics
-   **Concept**: A special triangle where trendlines **expand** outward rather than converge, resembling a **megaphone**.
-   **Structure**: Essentially a trading range characterized by continuously making higher highs and lower lows. A valid expanding triangle contains at least **5 reversals** (i.e., 5 legs).
-   **Core Behavior**: The core market behavior is **breakout failure**. Each time price makes a new high or low, it often quickly reverses, returning to the other end of the range.

### Trading Implications and Strategies

#### As a Major Reversal Pattern
-   **Condition**: When an expanding triangle is **large enough** (e.g., containing more than 20 bars) and clearly formed, it is usually a **major reversal** signal.
-   **Identification**:
    -   **Expanding Triangle Bottom**: Often appears as a **Lower Low Major Reversal**, a potential buying opportunity.
    -   **Expanding Triangle Top**: Often appears as a **Higher High Major Reversal**, a potential selling opportunity.
-   **Signal Enhancement**: If a strong, opposite-trend move appears within the pattern, it increases the probability of final reversal.

#### As a Continuation Pattern
-   **Context Determines Nature**: Its final direction depends on the market context in which it forms.
    -   **Expanding Triangle Bull Flag**: Appearing in an uptrend, usually a bullish signal, likely to break up.
    -   **Expanding Triangle Bear Flag**: Appearing in a downtrend, usually a bearish signal, likely to break down.

#### Trading Strategy
-   **Core Idea**: Follow the pattern's internal "breakout failure" characteristic, i.e., **"fade breakouts"**. Look for short signals near new highs, long signals near new lows.
-   **Entry Points**:
    -   **Conservative**: Wait for a **second entry signal** (such as small double top/bottom, wedge reversal) or wait for market to show **strong breakout** with good follow-through bars before entering.
    -   **Target**: First target is usually the other end of the pattern, or use **Measured Move (MM)** target.
-   **Stop**: When shorting, place stop above pattern high; when going long, place stop below pattern low.

## Pattern Reliability

### Nested Patterns
-   **Definition**: Refers to a smaller expanding triangle forming inside a larger expanding triangle.
-   **Significance**: This nested structure **enhances pattern reliability** and increases the probability of successful reversal.

### The Importance of Pattern Quality
-   **Standard Pattern**: A **clear, symmetrical** expanding triangle, with relatively balanced bar counts and amplitudes between legs, has higher success rate.
-   **Poor Pattern**: Patterns that are **asymmetric** (e.g., one leg too long or too short) or have chaotic structure are **unreliable**.
-   **Trading Principle**: Computer algorithms and institutional traders will **ignore** these "ugly" patterns. Without enough capital participation, the expected reversal won't occur. Therefore, **don't trade poor patterns**, as they have almost no predictive value.

## Summary Principles
-   **Identify Core Behavior**: The core of expanding triangles is choppy action and breakout failure; should sell at new highs and buy at new lows.
-   **Assess Size and Context**: Large, symmetrical patterns are potential **major reversal** signals; small patterns or patterns forming in strong trends are more likely to be **continuation flags**.
-   **Quality Determines Everything**: Only trade **clear, symmetrical** patterns. Ignore "ugly" patterns, as they are extremely unreliable.
-   **Utilize Nested Structure**: When nested patterns appear, pattern **reliability significantly increases**.
